The **Two-Pointer approach** is an optimal technique when solving problems that involve searching for pairs, triplets, or subarrays in a **linear data structure** (like an array or string) where the input is **sorted or can be sorted**.

It is used to significantly reduce the time complexity, typically from **O(N¬≤)** (which would be required for a brute-force nested loop) down to **O(N)** or **O(N log N)** (if sorting is required first).

<!-- truncate -->

### 1. üîç Finding Pairs or Subsets in Sorted Arrays (O(N) Time)

This is the classic use case, often called the **"Opposite Ends"** or **"Closing Pointers"** approach.

* **Goal:** Find a pair of elements that satisfy a condition (e.g., sum to a target, have a certain difference).
* **Setup:** One pointer starts at the **beginning (`left`)** and the other at the **end (`right`)**.
* **Movement:** The pointers move towards the middle based on the comparison:
    * If the result is too small, move `left` right (to increase the sum/value).
    * If the result is too large, move `right` left (to decrease the sum/value).
* **Example Problems:**
    * **Two Sum II (Sorted Array):** Find two numbers that sum to a target.
    * Finding a specific pair or triplet (e.g., 3Sum).

---

### 2. üß± Handling Boundaries (Trapping Rain Water) (O(N) Time)

This is a specific application where the pointers define an active area and are used to find the true limiting boundary without a pre-computation step.

* **Goal:** Calculate a value (like trapped water) that depends on the minimum of two dynamically changing boundaries (**Max Left** and **Max Right**). 
* **Setup:** Pointers start at opposite ends.
* **Movement:** Always move the pointer associated with the **smaller** of the two current maximum boundary values inward. This guarantees that the water level calculation at that position is finalized and correct.

### 3. üîÑ In-Place Reversal or Transformation (O(N) Time)

This is used when you need to perform an operation (like swapping) on corresponding elements from opposite sides of a list or string, often for reversal or palindrome checks.

* **Goal:** Modify a data structure **in-place** by relating elements from the front and back.
* **Setup:** Pointers start at opposite ends.
* **Movement:** Both pointers move inward **simultaneously** (after the operation): `left++` and `right--`.
* **Example Problems:**
    * **Reversing a string or array.**
    * **Checking if a string is a palindrome** (e.g., `aba`).
    * **Reversing only specific characters** (e.g., "Reverse Only Letters").
